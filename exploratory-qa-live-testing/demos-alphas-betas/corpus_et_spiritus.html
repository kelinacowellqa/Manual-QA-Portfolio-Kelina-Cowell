Introduction

This mini-project is part of my Exploratory QA & Live Testing work.

These projects focus on short, real-world testing scenarios across demos, early builds, live updates, and active indie games. The goal is not exhaustive coverage, but fast, practical QA judgement applied to current systems that may still be evolving.

This project is a targeted QA pass on Corpus Et Spiritus, tested on PC using build 2026.03.27.1. The focus was card resolution, card lifecycle behaviour, input handling, and player-facing clarity during rapid and repeated interactions.

Testing was charter-driven and risk-based. I focused on whether card actions resolved cleanly, whether rapid inputs could destabilise interaction behaviour, and whether the game clearly communicated card availability states such as playable vs recyclable.

I did not use Jira for this project. Bugs and findings were recorded directly in a structured QA workbook using session notes, a bug log, risk matrix, one-line summary, STAR summary, and player experience notes.

⭐ STAR Summary - Corpus Et Spiritus Targeted QA Pass

Situation: Short, self-directed QA mini-project on Corpus Et Spiritus, a card-based system game where player actions rely on card resolution, resource changes, and clear interaction states.

Task: Validate whether card interactions remained stable under sequential, rapid, and mixed inputs, with particular attention to click vs drag behaviour and card lifecycle clarity.

Action: Created a focused QA workbook and executed 2 targeted sessions. Testing started with card resolution under normal and rapid play, then escalated into repeated click and drag interactions to stress input handling and card state behaviour.

Result: Card execution remained stable under sequential and rapid play, with no unresolved actions or state breaks observed. One medium-severity input handling issue was logged where rapid clicking caused failed triggers and unintended multi-card activation on subsequent drag. A separate clarity finding was recorded around playable vs recyclable card states.

📈 Results

  • Completed 2 focused QA sessions on Corpus Et Spiritus.
  • Logged 1 medium-severity input handling bug with supporting video evidence.
  • Confirmed card resolution remained stable during sequential and rapid play.
  • Identified card lifecycle clarity as a player-facing weakness.
  • Confirmed no performance degradation, state desync, or permanent loss of control during tested conditions.

🧠 What I learned

  • Stable systems can still create player-facing uncertainty. Card resolution behaved consistently, but card availability states were not clearly explained.
  • Input pressure can reveal issues that normal play may miss. The card system behaved correctly under normal play, but rapid clicking exposed a conflict between click and drag interaction states.
  • Not every planned charter needs to stay in scope. The final test focus was adjusted to match the accessible systems and meaningful risks within the timebox.
  • Player understanding is part of system quality. If a mechanic works but the player cannot understand why, the experience still suffers.
  • Short QA passes still need traceability. Linking charters, sessions, bug reports, risk areas, and evidence made the project reviewable without turning it into a full case study.

🔚 Conclusion

This targeted QA pass on Corpus Et Spiritus found that card resolution is technically stable under the tested conditions. Cards resolved cleanly during sequential and rapid play, and no state breaks, unresolved actions, or permanent loss of control were observed.

The main defect identified was an input handling issue under rapid interaction. Repeated clicking caused cards to vibrate and fail to trigger, with later drag interactions sometimes failing or activating multiple cards unexpectedly.

The main player-facing weakness was clarity. Card lifecycle states, especially playable vs recyclable behaviour, were consistent but not clearly communicated, reducing player predictability and confidence during decision-making.

Overall, this project demonstrates a short, focused QA pass using risk-based scoping, evidence-backed bug reporting, and clear separation between technical defects and player experience findings.
